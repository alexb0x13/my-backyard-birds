Common Birds

With very little effort, you can lure birds to your backyard. You need to provide three simple elements: water, food and shelter.

Water - I use both elevated and ground-level birdbaths, as some birds, such as bluebirds, prefer to "bathe" on the ground. In droughty conditions you may see some uncommon birds, attracted by the water. One dry season an oriole and a scarlet tanager came to my birdbaths for a drink.

Drippers are an enticement, as birds love moving water. For years I used a commercial dripper, digging a channel to protect the tubing from lawn mowers and curious critters, but when the underground tubing sprung a leak, I resorted to a "haywire rig" (a Maine expression). A simple solution is gallon plastic milk jug hung on a shepherd's crook with the tiniest possible pinhole in the corner overhanging the birdbath. If you want to pretty it up, you can either paint over the label or apply a flower graphic, sealed with package tape.

Food – Food doesn't necessarily mean you need to put out feeders. There are many kinds of flowers, shrubs and trees that provide natural food. However, a feeder or two containing oil sunflower seed will also be appreciated by your feathered visitors. Suet feeders will attract woodpeckers, nuthatches and chickadees. Woodpeckers, nuthatches and other birds like commercial "nut blocks," however, when grackles and starlings are feeding their young, they will wipe them out in no time. Feeding birds is a rather fun ongoing trial and error process.

Shelter – birds feel more comfortable dining at feeders placed near to some kind of shelter. I have a row of forsythia bushes bordering one side of my property. Because of an aging and badly trimmed maple tree that sheds spindly branches in windy weather, it's easy for me to build a respectable brush pile at the foot of the maple. To keep the less wanted birds busy, I often sprinkle mixed birdseed or cracked corn over the brush pile. Each December I buy a "live" Christmas tree and secure it to a post directly in front of my patio doors. The birds love the security of this shelter and it provides many photo ops especially during a snowy winter.

Nesting birds are a special joy to observe. Although natural nesting is a bit out of your control, a wren house or bluebird house may entice those species to your yard. I must caution you that bluebirds require very special conditions (entrance to house should face a tree, tree shouldn't be mounted on a tree but on a post, etc.) but if successful, you will be rewarded by seeing these beautiful birds daily.

I suspect my large bridal wreath bush "houses" cardinals and probably house sparrows among others. Birds love the intricate tangle of its branches, and it's perfect for cardinals as they build their nests only about five feet from the ground. Mourning doves nest on the curve of my downspouts and other unlikely places.

As I have stated elsewhere, leave a bit of yard debris here and there. Last season's leaves, pine needles, acorns, etc. hide all kinds of goodies and it's fun to watch thrashers, towhees, many varieties of sparrows and others scratching and foraging for insects, spiders and lizards.

In addition to birds in a "casual" yard, you will see a few animals. Rabbits, chipmunks and squirrels (a special curse!) inhabit my yard. A family of red foxes made a den under my friend's shed – not that you would wish this to happen to you! She enjoys watching the kits venture out in the evening. Animal Control folks assure her they will move on when the kits are older. A "commune" of cottontail rabbits live beneath my yard barn. I don't grow petunias in my patio boxes any more, as one year I spotted a bunny sitting in the box, devouring them down to stubs. I must say, though, that when they recovered, they were the best petunias I've ever had.

Please nurture your feathered visitors and enjoy them!
